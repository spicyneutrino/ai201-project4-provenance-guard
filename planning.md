# Provenance Guard — Planning Document

## Context

Provenance Guard is designed for content platforms — publishing, academic submission, journalism, and creator communities — where knowing whether a piece was written by a human or generated by AI affects editorial decisions, trust, and policy enforcement. As AI writing tools become prevalent, platforms need a lightweight, transparent layer that can flag suspicious content without acting as a hard gate. This system provides probabilistic attribution rather than a binary verdict, preserving creator agency via an appeals path.

## Detection Signals

### Signal 1 — LLM Semantic Analysis (Groq)

**What it measures:** Semantic and stylistic patterns at the language model level. The LLM examines writing uniformity, hedging language ("it is important to note that"), passive voice patterns, structural formality, and absence of personal voice — all patterns that emerge when AI produces text.

**Output format:** A single float 0.0–1.0 (probability content is AI-generated). The text is truncated to 2000 characters to control token usage.

**Blind spot:** Polished human writing — academic essays, formal business prose — mimics AI output at the semantic level. A careful human writer who avoids colloquialisms will score high even if entirely human-authored.

### Signal 2 — Stylometric Heuristics (pure Python)

**What it measures:** Structural statistics over the raw text, independent of meaning:

1. **Sentence length variance** — AI tends to produce uniformly sized sentences. Low variance → high AI score.
   `score = 1 - min(variance / 400, 1.0)`

2. **Type-token ratio (TTR)** — AI text reuses vocabulary; high lexical diversity is a human marker. High TTR → low AI score.
   `TTR = unique_words / total_words; score = 1 - min(TTR / 0.7, 1.0)`

3. **Punctuation density** — Human informal writing uses more exclamation marks, em dashes, parenthetical asides. Low density → high AI score.
   `density = punctuation_chars / total_chars; score = 1 - min(density / 0.05, 1.0)`

Combined stylometric score = average of the three metric scores (0.0–1.0).

**Blind spot:** Formal academic human writing naturally has uniform sentence lengths, specialized vocabulary (reducing TTR), and minimal punctuation. Both signals agree it looks AI-generated even when it is not.

### Combining the Signals

```
combined_score = (0.60 × llm_score) + (0.40 × stylometric_score)
```

The LLM signal carries more weight (60%) because it captures semantic meaning that surface statistics cannot — a single sentence can have normal variance and TTR but still read as distinctly AI-generated in its phrasing. The stylometric signal (40%) provides a fast, deterministic sanity check that doesn't depend on API availability.

## Uncertainty Representation

A score of **0.5** means the two signals disagree or both land in the middle of their ranges. The system cannot confidently assign authorship. This is not treated as a positive detection — no action is taken, and the label explicitly tells the creator that no violation has been flagged.

**Thresholds:**

| Range | Attribution |
|---|---|
| score < 0.35 | `likely_human` |
| 0.35 ≤ score ≤ 0.65 | `uncertain` |
| score > 0.65 | `likely_ai` |

**Why 0.35 and 0.65?** These thresholds create a symmetric 30-point buffer around 0.5. A threshold of 0.5 would misclassify too many borderline cases; tighter bands (0.45/0.55) would create an uncertainty zone so narrow that normal signal noise pushes submissions in and out unpredictably. The 0.35/0.65 band is wide enough to absorb the combined uncertainty of two imperfect signals while still giving confident verdicts at the extremes.

## Transparency Label Variants

| Attribution | Label Text |
|---|---|
| `likely_human` | "This content shows strong indicators of human authorship. The writing style reflects the natural variation and personal voice consistent with human writing." |
| `uncertain` | "Our system was unable to confidently determine whether this content was written by a human or AI. This does not indicate a violation — many writing styles fall in this range. No action has been taken." |
| `likely_ai` | "This content shows strong indicators of AI generation. The writing style appears notably uniform and lacks the natural variation typical of human authors. If you wrote this yourself, you can submit an appeal below." |

## Appeals Workflow

**Who can submit:** Any creator who received a `likely_ai` label and believes the classification is wrong. The appeal endpoint is open (no authentication in this implementation); in production it would be gated by the creator's session token.

**Required fields:**
- `content_id` — the UUID returned from the original `/submit` call
- `creator_reasoning` — the creator's explanation of why the content is human-authored

**What the system does on receipt:**
1. Looks up `content_id` in the in-memory `content_store`; returns 404 if not found
2. Updates the in-memory status to `"under_review"`
3. Appends a NEW log entry (same `content_id`, `status: "under_review"`, `appeal_reasoning` populated) — the original classified entry is never mutated

**What a human reviewer sees in the audit log:**
Two entries sharing the same `content_id`: the original with `status: "classified"` and `confidence`/`attribution` values, and the appeal entry with `status: "under_review"` and `appeal_reasoning`. The reviewer can compare the original scores against the creator's stated reasoning and make a judgment call.

## Anticipated Edge Cases

### Edge Case 1: Formal Academic Human Writing

**Scenario:** A PhD student submits a paragraph from their dissertation. Sentence lengths are uniform (all 20–30 words), vocabulary is specialized reducing TTR, punctuation is minimal.

**Signal-level failure:** Stylometric signal scores it high AI (low variance, low TTR, low punctuation density). LLM signal may also score it high because formal academic prose structurally resembles AI output. Combined score may exceed 0.65 → `likely_ai` label on genuinely human content.

**Mitigation:** The appeals workflow exists specifically for this case. The `likely_ai` label text directly invites appeals: "If you wrote this yourself, you can submit an appeal below."

### Edge Case 2: Short Text (fewer than 3 sentences)

**Scenario:** A creator submits a two-sentence caption: "Great event last night. Loved every moment."

**Signal-level failure:** Sentence length variance requires at least 2 sentences to compute; with only 2 sentences the variance is meaningful but unstable. TTR on short text is artificially high (few repeated words), pushing the AI score down. The stylometric signal becomes unreliable at low sample sizes.

**Implementation handling:** When fewer than 2 sentences are detected, the variance score defaults to `1.0` (assumes uniformity, i.e., worst case AI-like). This is a conservative bias that may over-flag short text.

## Architecture

```
Submission Flow:
POST /submit
    |
    v
[Signal 1: Groq LLM] ---------> llm_score (0.0-1.0)
    |
[Signal 2: Stylometric] ------> stylometric_score (0.0-1.0)
    |
[Combiner: 0.6*llm + 0.4*stylo] --> combined_score
    |
[Label Generator] --> attribution + label_text
    |
[Audit Log Writer] --> audit_log.json
    |
JSON Response

Appeal Flow:
POST /appeal
    |
content_store lookup (404 if missing)
    |
Update status -> "under_review"
    |
[Audit Log Writer: appeal entry] --> audit_log.json
    |
JSON Response (message, status)
```
